\subsection{Neuron Automaton Results}


Using random initial conditions in the neuron automation usually led to either the whole system eventually dying out and being inert in the "ready" condition or to stable, infinitely forward moving gliders appearing. An example of a random initial pattern evolving into stable gliders is shown in figure \ref{fig:random}. On this and other neuron automata figures black indicates the ready state, red indicates the firing state and white indicates the resting state. 

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{figs/neuron/random.png}
    \caption{1) a random initial condition for the automation 2) system after evolving into a stable state with two infinitely moving gliders.}
    \label{fig:random}
\end{figure}

By experimenting, several initial conditions were constructed that would lead to interesting developments of the system such as a periodic oscillator or a pattern that infinitely generates more moving patterns. Some interesting initial conditions are pictured below. 

First a number of infinitely moving gliders were identified. Those kinds of patterns move in a set direction unless they collide with another pattern. Since the grid boundary conditions are periodical the infinite movement is represented by periodically crossing one boundary and appearing on another. On figure \ref{fig:gliders} are brought three such examples. 

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{figs/neuron/gliders.png}
    \caption{Three examples of infinitely moving patterns. Two patterns on the left move in the northern direction and the one on the right moves diagonally to south-east.}
    \label{fig:gliders}
\end{figure}

By having two simple gliders collide with eachother using initial conditions shown in figure \ref{fig:bouncer} it is possible to create two new gliders that move at a right angle to the initial motion giving the impression of two bouncing gliders.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{figs/neuron/bouncer.png}
    \caption{Initial condition that leads to two gliders bouncing in a 90 degree angle}
    \label{fig:bouncer}
\end{figure}

It is also possible to set up a pattern that stays in the same location and oscillates infinitely. An oscillator with three distinct steps was identified that is pictured in figure \ref{fig:oscillator}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{figs/neuron/oscillator.png}
    \caption{A three step oscillating pattern}
    \label{fig:oscillator}
\end{figure}

On figure \ref{fig:rake} is pictured a pattern that moves forward and periodically creates more moving patterns.
