\subsection{$p$-Orbits}


Figure~\ref{fig:p_orbits_1} displays the period-1 points found by solving
\(F(x,y)=(x,y)\) for \(\varepsilon=0.3\), \(r_1=3.1\), and different values of
\(r_2\). For the symmetric case, \(r_1=r_2=3.1\), we receive the fixed point that has been predicted by theory:  
\[
x=y=1-\frac{1}{3.1}.
\]
One can also verify that, when one increases \(r_2\) and \(r_1\) is kept fixed, the fixed point moves away
from the diagonal \(x=y\) (see cases b, c and d). In terms of physical interpretation, it is to be expected since now the growth parameter
differs between both populations. The second population grows more rapidly, which can be observed by the stronger 
increase in the \(y\)-coordinate.

Figure~\ref{fig:p_orbits_2} shows the points that satisfy
\[
F^2(x,y)=(x,y)
\]
with minimal period 2. This minimal condition, as observed previously, is fundamental for the study
and identification of genuine period-p points. Although all fixed points also satisfy \(F^2(x,y)=(x,y)\), they do not 
count as genuine period-2 orbits as they already have period 1. For the symmetric case \(r_1=r_2=3.1\), the period-2 points

\[
(0.76456652,0.76456652)
\quad \text{and} \quad
(0.55801413,0.55801413),
\]
are found. This explains why the phase portrait for the same parameters shows two attracting locations rather than
convergence to the period-1 fixed point.

For all investigated assymetric cases, \(r_2>r_1\), two fixed points were found and observed to move away from the diagonal,
where the displacement increases with \(r_2\). One can easily observe this comparing \(r_2=3.55\) and \(r_2=3.8\), where the separation between the two points becomes larger. 




\subsubsection{$3$-orbits}
Unlike initially expected, no genuine period-3 orbits were detected.
The only period-3 orbits that were detected were the fixed points, which satisfy \(F^3(x,y)=(x,y)\) naturally. However, their minimal period is 1 and thus these cannot be counted as genuine period-3 orbits.
