\subsection{$p$-Orbits}


Figure~\ref{fig:p_orbits_1} displays the period-1 points found by solving
\(F(x,y)=(x,y)\) for \(\varepsilon=0.3\), \(r_1=3.1\), and different values of
\(r_2\). For the symmetric case, \(r_1=r_2=3.1\), which goes accordingly with the theoretical
prediction, where 
\[
x=y=1-\frac{1}{3.1}.
\]
One can also verify that, when one increases \(r_2\) and \(r_1\) is kept fixed, the fixed point moves away
from the diagonal \(x=y\). In terms of physical interpretation, it is to be expected since now the growth parameter
differs between both populations. The second population grows more rapidly, which can be observed by the stronger 
increase in the \(y\)-coordinate.

The existence of these fixes points does not, however, describe the long-term behavior of the system.
For that, one has to further study their stability, which can be achieve through the following period-p
points and the respective bifurcation diagram.


\subsubsection{$2$-orbits}

Figure~\ref{fig:p_orbits_2} shows the points that satisfy
\[
F^2(x,y)=(x,y)
\]
with minimal period 2. This minimal condition, as observed previously, is fundamental for the study
and identification of genuine period-p points. Although they satisfy \(F^2(x,y)=(x,y)\), they do not 
count as genuine period-2 orbits. For the symmetric case \(r_1=r_2=3.1\), two period points are found,

\[
(0.76456652,0.76456652)
\quad \text{and} \quad
(0.55801413,0.55801413),
\]

explains why the phase portrait for the same parameters shows two attracting locations rather than
convergence to the period-1 fixed point.

For the assymetric case, \(r_2>r_1\), two fixed points were found and observed to move away from the diagonal,
where the displacement increases with \(r_2\). One can easily observe this comparing \(r_2=3.55\) and \(r_2=3.8\), where the separation between the two points becomes larger. This is consistent with the bifurcation diagram, where the single branch splits into two and four branches, respectively.

\subsubsection{$3$-orbits}
Following the project instructions, unlike initially expected, no genuine period-3 orbits were detected.
This is a very particular case where it was observed that every fixed point satisfies \(F^3(x,y)=(x,y)\), and none is minimal, so those cannot be counted as period-3 orbits.


\subsubsection{$4$-orbits}

Following the given suggestion, annother period was tested, period-4. Which was found to describe the behavior 
of the system as desired. Figure~\ref{fig:p_orbits_4} shows a genuine period-4 orbit for
\((r_1,r_2,\varepsilon)=(3.1,3.8,0.3)\). This now goes along with the bifurcation diagram that exhibits four points 
at approximately the same value of \(r_2\).
