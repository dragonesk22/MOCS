



In this project, a discrete-time model of two coupled logistic maps was studied. This was done in order to understand how the long-term behaviour of the system changes with the growth parameters. The numerical results show that the system can exhibit several different dynamical regimes, ranging from convergence to periodic orbits to increasingly complex, apparently chaotic behaviour.

For the symmetric case $r_1=r_2=3.1$, the theoretical non-trivial fixed point was found at 
\[
x=y=1-\frac{1}{3.1}=0.67741935.
\]
Although this fixed point exists mathematically, the numerical simulations show that it is not attractive for the chosen parameters. Instead, trajectories converge to a stable period-2 orbit. This illustrates an important feature of nonlinear dynamical systems: the existence of a fixed point does not necessarily mean that it determines the observed long-term dynamics.

When the symmetry between the two populations is broken by increasing $r_2$ while keeping $r_1=3.1$, the system displays richer behaviour. For moderate values of $r_2$, the stable period-2 orbit persists but becomes asymmetric, with the two populations no longer following identical values. For larger values, such as $r_2=3.8$, the simulations show a transition to a stable period-4 orbit. Finally, for still higher values such as $r_2=3.95$, the bifurcation diagram and phase portraits suggest more complicated behaviour, with many possible long-term values forming a point cloud.

Overall, the different numerical methods used in the lab give a consistent picture. The computation of periodic points, the bifurcation diagrams, and the phase portraits all indicate the same progression: as the growth parameter increases, the coupled logistic system moves from simple periodic behaviour to higher-period orbits and eventually to complex dynamics. This confirms that even a relatively simple coupled population model can produce rich nonlinear behaviour, including bifurcations and possible chaotic regimes.
